#1 Origins
Where the pattern engine comes from

When I was fifteen I picked up Stephen Hawking's A Brief History of Time. I did not understand most of it. But one thing landed and never left: we are sitting on a rock orbiting an unremarkable star in one of a hundred billion galaxies, and the piece of reality we can actually observe is so small it rounds to nothing. Everything we think we know is built on a fraction of the data.

That idea did something to how I process things. Not in an intellectual way. In a mechanical way. It installed a default setting: your first read on any situation is almost certainly incomplete. Do not trust it. Keep looking.

I wanted to be an astronomer. Then a pilot. Then I studied aerospace engineering and spent my time building drones. Then COVID came and wiped all of those paths off the table simultaneously. No aviation jobs. No engineering openings. Nothing.

So I did marketing for a bar in Bangalore. Promoting events. Getting bodies through the door. Learning, without knowing I was learning, how to make a stranger feel like they should be here instead of somewhere else.

Before all of that, I was captaining an esports team. Critical Ops, PUBG Mobile, Call of Duty Mobile. Top 6 in Asia. Tournaments where the whole thing turns on a four-second decision and five people are screaming in your ear and you have to process all of it and commit to a call and live with whatever happens next. That is not a metaphor for sales. That is sales. The same cognitive operation running on different inputs.

Later I built a motorcycle community and started racing on tracks. The thing about a corner at speed is that the moment you hesitate, you crash. You pick a line, commit, and hold nerve while everything in your body says brake. If you brake, you run wide and you are done. If you hold, you carry speed through and come out the other side faster than you went in.

Hawking taught me to observe. Esports taught me to decide under pressure. Racing taught me to commit. The bar taught me to read a room. None of it was training for sales. All of it was training for sales. I just did not know it until I picked up a phone for the first time and realised the whole thing is the same game played at different frame rates.

#2 Contact
The first three seconds

Someone picks up. They say a word. Sometimes just a syllable. "Yeah?" or "Hello?" or their name. That single sound is the most information-dense moment on the call and almost everyone blows past it because they are busy thinking about what they are going to say next.

Here is what is running in my head before I respond:

Tone. Rushed, relaxed, annoyed, curious, flat. A rushed tone gives me maybe 15 seconds before they cut me off. Relaxed gives me 45. Annoyed means they were already having a bad moment before I dialled and everything I say is filtered through that.

Environment. Background noise. Wind means driving. I have a narrow window and they cannot focus. Quiet means a desk. More room to operate.

Speed of pickup. Fast answer usually means they expected a different call. Slow answer means they almost did not pick up at all. Both tell me something about how much permission I have.

All of this happens before I open my mouth. It is not analysis. It is pattern matching. The same way a chess player recognises a position before calculating anything, I have heard enough first words to know, before the syllable finishes, what kind of conversation I am in.

This matters because my next sentence is not predetermined. It is selected from a short list based on what I just heard. A rushed person gets a tight, direct entry. A relaxed person gets a human one. An annoyed person gets acknowledged.

Decision: first response
IF
Rushed + fast pickup → Direct. Name, reason, one line. Ask if now is bad. Do not build rapport they do not want.
IF
Relaxed + quiet → Slow down. Be a person. Let the first ten seconds breathe. The rapport is the runway and they are giving you permission to build it.
IF
Annoyed or hostile → Acknowledge. "Sounds like I caught you at a bad time." Give them the exit. The ones who stay after you hand them the door are the only ones worth talking to.
IF
Confused → Context immediately. You have three seconds before confusion becomes "not interested" as a reflex rather than a decision.

That is what I mean when I say intention over approach. The approach changes every call. The intention does not. The intention is always the same: figure out who I am talking to before I decide what to say.

#3 Signals
Reading the room through a phone line

No body language. No eye contact. No arms crossed or uncrossed. One channel. Audio. And inside that single channel there is more data than most people realise.

The thing I listen for hardest is not what someone says. It is the gap between what they say and how they say it.

"We are okay right now" in a flat, even tone with no hesitation means they are okay. Move on. But "we are okay... right now" with a pause before "okay" or a rising inflection at the end or a small exhale after the sentence, that is a completely different piece of information. That means they are telling themselves they are okay. There is a crack. And if I ask the right question, what is actually true comes through.

A fast "not interested" is a genuine not interested. A slow "not interested" with a qualifier attached ("not interested right now" or "not interested, we just bought something") is not a no. It is an explanation. People only explain when they feel the need to justify their position. Which means the position is not as solid as the words make it sound.

Chris Voss calls the underlying skill "tactical empathy" in Never Split the Difference. He built it negotiating with hostage takers for the FBI. The principle transfers perfectly to a cold call: the person on the other end is broadcasting their internal state through micro-signals in their voice, and if you can read those signals and reflect them back, they feel heard. A person who feels heard tells you things they did not plan to say.

I am not decoding every pause consciously. After a few thousand calls the recognition runs automatically. The same way a musician stops hearing individual notes and starts hearing phrases, I stop hearing words and start hearing emotional shapes. The call has a contour. A rhythm. And the rhythm tells you whether you are heading toward a conversation or a hang-up long before either one arrives.

#4 Nothing
The silence war

The hardest thing I have learned in sales is to stop talking. Not pause. Stop. Let the line go dead for four, five, six seconds while everything in me screams to fill it with something. A clarification. A redirect. A nervous laugh. Anything. I do not. I let the silence sit there like a living thing between me and the other person.

Second 1. They finished talking. My instinct says respond. I override it.

Second 2. The silence registers for them. They notice I have not said anything. This is unusual. Salespeople talk constantly.

Second 3. Tension builds. Both sides. This is where most people crack. If I crack, I lose the frame. If they crack, they fill the gap with something real.

Second 4-5. They talk. What comes out in this moment, the thing someone reaches for to fill an uncomfortable silence, is almost never rehearsed. It is the truth. Not the polite answer. The real one.

Second 6. If they still have not spoken, I come back soft. Not a pitch. A question. Something that opens a door without pushing them through it.

Silence feels like failure because every sales training in the world says keep the conversation moving. But movement and progress are not the same thing. A call that moves fast usually goes nowhere. A call that pauses at the right moment goes deep. Deep is where the meetings live.

The esports analogy is accurate. The best competitive players are not the ones who fire the most. They hold position. They wait. They take the shot when the angle opens. In racing, the fastest lap is never the one where you accelerate hardest. It is the one where you brake at exactly the right point and carry speed through the corner. In both, the instinct says go. The discipline says wait. The result comes from the wait.

#5 Fork
The pivot moment

Between 20 and 45 seconds in, every cold call forks. The prospect says something. It is either a real objection, a reflexive brush-off, or a clue wearing the costume of a rejection. The whole call depends on which one you identify it as. You have about two seconds.

Decision: is this a real no?
IF
"We already have something." — Information, not rejection. They just told me about their world. The move is curiosity: what do they have, how does it work, what would they change. They opened a door. Walk through it.
IF
"Send me an email." — Exit strategy, not interest. That email will never be read. But the request tells me they are polite, not hostile. They are unconvinced, not opposed. The move: "What specifically would you want the email to cover? If you can name it, I will send it. If you cannot, the email probably will not help either."
IF
"Not in the market right now." — Timing. Usually genuine. But "right now" is the key phrase. They did not say never. The move is to offer value without asking: "Totally fair. Can I ask one thing before I go, just so I know whether it is worth circling back?"
IF
"I do not have time." — Maybe true. Maybe a brush-off. Test it: "No problem. Is there a better day?" A real busy person gives you a day. A brush-off says "just call whenever" and never picks up again.

Pivots are not comebacks. I am not "overcoming objections." I am reading the signal underneath the words and responding to the signal. The words say "send me an email." The signal says "I am not convinced this is worth my time." The script answers the words. Intention answers the signal. Two completely different responses to the same sentence.

Matt Dixon and Brent Adamson circled something adjacent in The Challenger Sale. Their data showed the top reps were not relationship builders. They were reframers. People who brought a perspective the prospect had not considered. What the book does not fully say is that you cannot reframe something you do not understand. The reframe only lands if you spent the first 30 seconds actually listening instead of waiting for your turn to pitch.

#6 Mirror
The echo

When a prospect says something real, I repeat their exact words back to them. Not a paraphrase. Not a reframe. Their words. If they say "the footage thing is a nightmare," I say "the footage thing is a nightmare." Full stop. Then silence.

Paraphrasing says "I understood you." Echoing says "I heard you." Those are different things. On a cold call nobody needs to be understood. They need to be heard. That is a lower bar and it is the bar that earns the next 30 seconds.

Mechanically, the echo does something else too. While they respond to their own words bounced back (and they always do, usually by expanding), I am running the next decision: is this pain worth exploring, or is this small talk? The echo buys me two to three seconds of processing time disguised as active listening.

Chris Voss calls it mirroring. Keenan, in Gap Selling, gets at the same thing from a different angle: the gap between where the prospect is and where they want to be has to be described in their language for it to feel real. If I use my vocabulary, it is a pitch. If I use theirs, it is a shared observation. Same content. Completely different effect. The echo is how I collect their vocabulary in real time and feed it back.

There is a deeper shift that happens. When someone hears their exact words reflected, the dynamic tips. It stops feeling like a sales call. It starts feeling like a conversation. The rehearsed deflections fall away. The real stuff starts coming through. Not because I tricked anyone. Because I made a space where honesty was easier than performing.

#7 Retreat
The pull-away

After describing a set of problems that people in their position commonly deal with, after I have watched for the reaction, after they have claimed a pain or shown a flicker of recognition, I do not lean in. I lean out.

"I get the feeling this probably is not relevant to you."

That sentence, said casually and meant genuinely, is the most powerful line I have. It does the opposite of what sales training says to do. It hands the prospect an exit.

Here is what happens on their end: the pressure disappears. For the first time on the call, nobody is selling them anything. They have a real choice. And when people have a genuine choice, something interesting happens. They lean in. "No actually, the footage thing really is a problem for us." They are now arguing for their own need. I did not push them there. I created the space and they walked into it on their own.

If they take the exit, that is equally good. Because now I know this is a real no, not a polite deflection, and I stop spending time on something that was never going to happen.

The pull-away only works if the intention behind it is real. If you say "I get the feeling this is not relevant" while hoping they argue, they can hear the manipulation in your voice. The tone gives you away. You have to genuinely mean it. You have to be actually okay with the conversation ending right there. That is the hard part. Every instinct in sales says pursue, persist, never stop. But the data is clear: giving people permission to leave is the thing that makes the most of them stay.

Every interaction earns the right to the next one. Nothing is owed.

#8 Release
When to let go

Most calls are not meetings. The skill that separates the reps who last from the ones who burn out is knowing when to stop.

There is a moment on a dying call where you can feel the energy leaving. The answers get shorter. The pauses get longer, but not in the thinking way. In the waiting-for-you-to-finish way. Most reps push harder here. More features. More urgency. It never works. You are putting coins into a machine that is already unplugged.

Decision: continue or release?
IF
Answers shrinking, energy dropping → They are done. Respect it. "I can hear you are busy. Appreciate the time. If this ever comes up, I am easy to find." Leave clean. They remember how you ended, not what you pitched.
IF
"Let me think about it" with no next step → Slow no. Flush it: "Totally fair. Just so I know, is this a not-now or a not-ever? Either is genuinely fine." The ones who say not-ever were done anyway. The ones who say not-now just told you when to come back.
IF
They name a problem you cannot solve → Do not pretend. "Honestly I do not think we are the right fit for that." Credibility earned by walking away from one deal follows you to the next fifty.

Letting go is the hardest discipline. It is also the most valuable. Every minute on a dead call is a minute not spent on the person three dials away who actually has the problem you solve.

#9 Source
The source code

I work a night shift from Bangalore. 6pm to 2am, five nights a week, calling into a country I have never been to, talking to people I will probably never meet in person. Before the shift I ride motorcycles. After the shift I sleep or I do not and the phone rings again the next evening and I do it again.

People ask why. The honest answer is that I love the puzzle. Not in a motivational way. The way a chess player loves a board. Every call is a new position. Incomplete information, a ticking clock, a series of micro-decisions that compound. Sometimes it is a meeting. Sometimes it is a hang-up. But the process, the act of reading a stranger through a phone line and figuring out whether there is a way to be useful to them, that is the part I find endlessly interesting.

The connection to Hawking is not a reach. It is how I genuinely think about the work. Every call is an observation of a tiny slice of someone's reality. I cannot see the whole picture. I never will. But if I watch carefully, if I listen without assumptions, if I hold silence when instinct says talk, sometimes a pattern emerges that tells me something true about what this person needs. When that happens, it stops being a sales call and starts being a real exchange between two people trying to figure something out.

That is the source code. Not the script. Not the sequence. Not the cadence or the AI-graded opener score. The source code is the intention to understand before you speak. To observe before you act. To treat every "hello?" as a new dataset and meet it with genuine curiosity about what you might find.

Everything else, the scripts, the tools, the frameworks, is the interface layer. It matters. But it is not the engine.

The engine is whether, at 11:30pm on a Tuesday in Bangalore, when a stranger in another timezone picks up the phone and says "yeah?", your first impulse is to pitch or to listen.

If it is to listen, you are running the right code.

If it is to pitch, nothing else will save you.
